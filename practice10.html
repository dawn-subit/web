    1. Composite pseudotachylyte-ultracataclasite veins at the Main Central Thrust (central Nepal): formation conditions, fabrics, and fabric-forming processes

    Pseudotachylyte can be easily overseen and hard to observe in the field that needs a meticulous observation. Field observation shows Pseudotachylyte as a thin-thick band that often acquainted by parallel to oblique to cross-cutting veins. Fragmentation, grain size, and chemical analysis along with petrography were conducted to better understand the process of formation, fragmentation and its intermixing processes. This research reported pseudotachylytes on the vicinity of Main Central Thrust for the first time. Instantaneous lab work and analysis confirmed repeated seismic activity along with repeated fragmentation and mixing activities. While this study confirms repeated seismic slip events, the individual slip times and precise slip rates could not be constrained. This crucial discovery opens new avenues for research, suggesting that similar seismogenic features may be preserved in other segments of the MCT and major Himalayan fault zones, awaiting systematic exploration.

    2. Structure evolution of the Kathmandu Nappe, Insight through the thrust and fault belt at Galchhi

    Galchhi lies west of Kathmandu, south of the Main Central Thrust (MCT). It spans the contact between the Higher Himalayan Kathmandu Nappe and the Lesser Himalayan rocks, confirming your described "trans-boundary location". To unravel the complex history of exhumation, subduction, and erosion, this study employed rigorous geological and structural mapping, supported by petrographic and microstructural analysis. The work confirms the area experienced poly-metamorphic phases and multistage deformation, a characteristic feature of the complex tectonometamorphic evolution of the central Nepal Himalaya. Key findings include the identification of a granitic intrusion along the Mahesh Khola section, with related dynamics responsible for microstructures such as mica blasts, pegmatite veins, and new mineral growth including clinozoisite and titanite. A significant discovery was the correction of a long-held mineral identification, where observed grains were determined to be clinozoisite, not sillimanite. Microstructural analysis proved critical: quartz c-axis fabric analysis was used to constrain deformation temperatures, while garnet porphyroblasts preserving inclusion trails were analyzed via the Foliation Inflection/Intersection Axis (FIA) method to identify relicts of episodic deformation. My research in central Nepal integrated field geological and structural mapping with laboratory microstructural analysis. We analyzed quartz C-axis fabrics to constrain deformation temperatures and strain geometry. A critical re-evaluation of minerals in the Galchhi area revealed that phases previously identified as sillimanite are, in fact, clinozoisite (epidote group), occurring as randomly arranged blasts. Furthermore, we measured rotational angles and inflection axes of garnet porphyroblasts to decipher multiple deformational events preserved within them

    3. Engineering and geo-technical properties of the Quartenary Sediment

    The Kathmandu Valley, encompassing the capital and surrounding districts, is a structural basin resting on a Precambrian metasedimentary and metamorphic basement. This basin is filled with a thick succession of Quaternary lacustrine (lake) deposits. The surficial stratigraphy across much of the valley is dominated by these sediments, primarily composed of clay, silt, and organic mud. The most distinctive unit within this sequence is the locally known "Kalimati" (black clay). This deposit, often several meters thick, is characterized by its dark black to grey color, which results from a high content of organic material intermixed with fine-grained clays and silts. Drilling and excavation-based research has confirmed the widespread distribution and variable depth of this clay unit across the valley. Given the foundational role this clay plays for infrastructure in the Kathmandu Valley, a geotechnical study was conducted to characterize its properties. Disturbed and undisturbed samples were collected from various locations across the valley, from depths of up to 6 meters. Laboratory analyses focused on consolidation behavior and clay activity. The study revealed that despite a relatively low overall clay mineral content, the Kalimati clay exhibits a high water-holding capacity and significant activity. This behavior is attributed to the presence of abundant smectite-group minerals, which are highly expansive. Notably, the geotechnical properties of this organic-rich, silty clay show significant parallels to well-documented expansive clays such as Houston clay. The high smectite content directly leads to pronounced swelling and shrinkage potential, posing a substantial hazard for engineering projects. This necessitates careful geotechnical evaluation and precautionary design for foundations and earthworks. In contrast, studies of clayey sediments in other Nepalese basins, such as the Pokhara Valley, show markedly different geotechnical behavior, highlighting the unique and challenging nature of the Kalimati clay.
